# Di - Informational Density

## Working English Version

This document presents a public and methodological definition of informational density as used within the NeoMundi framework.

It does not disclose production coefficients, proprietary parameters, thresholds, internal weights, full decision rules, or runtime inference mechanisms used in the NeoMundi engine.

## Definition

Informational density refers to a relative reading of the concentration, transformation, or dissipation of information observed during an AI generation or an execution segment.

It aims to qualify the relationship between the information produced, the generated volume, the informational effort involved, and the associated level of risk or uncertainty.

Within the NeoMundi framework, Di should not be understood as an absolute measure of truth or quality.

It is a methodological indicator intended to observe how a generation transforms and stabilizes information within a given context.

## Role of Informational Density

Informational density makes it possible to go beyond simple volumetry.

Two responses may have a similar volume while presenting different informational densities.

Di helps distinguish between:

- a long response with little useful information;
- a short but highly concentrated response;
- a dense and stable response;
- a dense but risky response;
- a verbose response with low informational value;
- a response that mobilizes significant informational effort for a result that is not very usable.

## Interpretation

High informational density may indicate a rich, concentrated, or highly structured response.

However, high density is not automatically positive.

A response may be dense while still being unstable, misleading, or insufficiently verified.

Low informational density may indicate a diffuse, repetitive, or information-poor response.

However, low density is not automatically negative.

Some pedagogical, narrative, or explanatory tasks may require more space, more context, and therefore a lower apparent density.

## What Di Does Not Claim to Measure

Di does not claim to measure:

- absolute semantic truth;
- factual certainty;
- the complete human quality of a response;
- governance stability by itself;
- the certain presence or absence of hallucination;
- the model’s internal understanding;
- the legal responsibility of a generation;
- real physical energy.

Di measures a conventional informational relationship, not a universal truth.

## Relationship with Volumetry

Volumetry observes the volumes being handled.

Di introduces a more interpretive reading of how these volumes are transformed into usable, dense, diffuse, or dissipated information.

Volumetry answers the question:

How much information was handled?

Di contributes to answering the question:

What informational form does this production appear to take?

## Relationship with Dv

Dv observes the relationship between output volume and input volume.

Di observes relative informational density.

A response may have strong volumetric expansion but low informational density if it repeats, dilutes, or over-explains.

A response may have low volumetric expansion but high informational density if it strongly concentrates information.

## Relationship with E

E observes an informational load or effort.

Di observes a density or informational relationship associated with that load.

E and Di should be interpreted together.

High informational energy may be relevant if it corresponds to useful informational density.

It may be problematic if it corresponds to high informational expenditure for low usable value.

## Relationship with G and ΔG

Di does not replace G.

G observes a governance stability state.

ΔG observes a variation in stability over time.

Di observes relative informational density.

These signals may be correlated, but they do not measure the same thing.

A generation may be dense but unstable.

A generation may be stable but not very dense.

A generation may be long, dense, and stable.

A generation may be long, diffuse, and unstable.

This is precisely the value of a multi-signal reading.

## Use Within a Governance Framework

In a runtime governance system, Di may contribute to:

- contextualizing the informational quality of a generation;
- identifying verbose or diluted responses;
- detecting highly concentrated responses that may require verification;
- complementing stability signals;
- enriching audit analyses;
- providing an additional layer of observability.

Di should not trigger a governance decision on its own.

It should be interpreted alongside other signals, the business context, applied thresholds, and the expected risk level.

## Methodological Position

Informational density is an informational observability metric.

It aims to make visible a dimension often ignored in generative systems: the way a response transforms volume into information that is more or less dense, usable, or dissipated.

It does not claim to provide an absolute quality score.

It is intended to enrich interpretation, not to replace human judgment or other governance signals.

## Limits

Informational density is highly context-dependent.

A good legal, medical, scientific, or pedagogical response may require different density depending on the use case.

A very dense response may be difficult to interpret.

A low-density response may be intentionally pedagogical.

Di must therefore always be read within its usage context.

Production parameters, internal coefficients, and integration rules within the NeoMundi engine belong to the proprietary implementation and are not documented in this public repository.
