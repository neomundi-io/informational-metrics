# E - Informational Energy

## Working English Version

This document presents a public and methodological definition of informational energy as used within the NeoMundi framework.

It does not disclose production coefficients, proprietary parameters, thresholds, internal weights, full decision rules, or runtime inference mechanisms used in the NeoMundi engine.

## Definition

Informational energy refers to a conventional estimate of the informational effort mobilized or dissipated during an AI generation or an execution segment.

It does not correspond to physical energy measured in joules.

It is a methodological unit of observation intended to qualify the informational load associated with a generative output.

## Role of Informational Energy

Informational energy makes it possible to observe that an AI generation does not only produce a volume of text.

It also mobilizes an effort of processing, organization, stabilization, and transformation of information.

Within the NeoMundi framework, E aims to make this dimension of informational effort observable.

It may help distinguish between:

- a simple generation with low informational load;
- a long but controlled generation;
- a complex generation requiring greater stabilization;
- a generation that may be costly in informational processing;
- a response that absorbs a higher level of uncertainty or risk.

## Interpretation

Low informational energy may correspond to a short, simple, or low-demand generation.

High informational energy may correspond to a long, complex, uncertain, or more costly-to-stabilize generation.

E should never be interpreted in isolation.

A high value of E is not necessarily negative.  
It may indicate a complex task, a detailed response, or a rich informational context.

A low value of E is not necessarily positive.  
It may also correspond to an insufficient, poor, or overly rapid response.

## What E Does Not Claim to Measure

E does not claim to measure:

- actual electrical consumption;
- physical energy;
- semantic truth;
- factual certainty;
- the human quality of a response;
- governance stability by itself;
- the absence of hallucination;
- the legal responsibility of a generation.

E measures a conventional informational load, not truth.

## Relationship with Volumetry

Volumetry describes the informational volume being handled.

E introduces a more qualitative reading of the informational effort associated with that volume.

Two responses may have comparable volumetry but different informational energy profiles depending on their complexity, level of uncertainty, or exposure to risk.

## Relationship with Dv

Dv observes the volumetric transformation between input and output.

E observes the informational effort associated with the generation.

A response may show high volumetric density without necessarily being unstable.  
Another may show lower volumetry but a more critical informational load.

Dv and E should therefore be interpreted as two complementary dimensions.

## Relationship with G and ΔG

E does not replace G.

G observes a governance stability state.  
ΔG observes a variation in stability over time.  
E observes an informational load or effort.

These signals may be correlated, but they do not measure the same thing.

## Methodological Position

Informational energy is an observability metric.

It aims to enrich the reading of generative systems by showing that a response cannot be reduced to its length or stability score.

It introduces a cautious informational-thermodynamic reading, without claiming to directly measure a physical quantity.

## Limits

Informational energy must be interpreted within a defined methodological framework.

It depends on the context, the type of task, the generated volume, the observed risk level, and the modeling choices retained.

Production parameters, internal coefficients, and integration rules within the NeoMundi engine belong to the proprietary implementation and are not documented in this public repository.
